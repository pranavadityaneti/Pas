Merchant Agreements — Compliance Review

Pick At Store (PAS Retail Networks Pvt. Ltd.) · 2026-06-15 · three agreements: Grocery (2%), Other Stores (5%), Restaurant (5%/7%)

NOT LEGAL ADVICE. This is a structured, non-lawyer review (engineering + compliance lens) to hand to qualified Indian legal counsel and a chartered accountant. Every item below is flagged for their confirmation, not asserted as legal fact. Several findings were verified directly in the codebase and are noted as such.
Bottom line. The agreements are a competent B2B skeleton — clear intermediary status, indemnity, liability cap, a named grievance officer, and a real electronic-execution audit trail. But they were drafted before today's tax/fee decisions, and in four places the live system contradicts the signed text on money. Two of those are exposures to fix before the first real settlement and before more merchants sign: (1) the 1.9% gateway fee is authorized nowhere in the signed document, and (2) the commission engine's default rate (8%/10%) exceeds the signed restaurant/bakery rate (5%/7%).

1 · Today's tax/fee decisions vs the signed text

Of the four charges decided today, only the commission itself is clearly on the signed page. The rest range from absent to thin-but-arguable — and none are implemented in the settlement engine yet.

CRITICAL · ABSENT1.9% payment-gateway fee borne by the merchant

No clause anywhere authorizes it. §23 (Payment Services) only disclaims PAS liability for gateway failures; the §3.5/§3.7 settlement clause deducts "other agreed charges," but that term is undefined and the 1.9% is quantified nowhere in the signed document — so it is not an "agreed" charge against any merchant. The commission (2/5/7%) is stated precisely; a fee that's ~25–95% as large is invisible. Verified in code: the net formula omits any gateway term and there's no gateway column in the schema — so it's neither papered nor implemented.

Fix: Add an explicit "Payment Processing Fee" clause in COMMERCIAL TERMS (1.9% of gross, deductible at settlement, pass-through of aggregator charges, exclusive of taxes), and add it to the §3.5/§3.7 deduction list. Until re-signed, deducting 1.9% on already-signed agreements is a live unauthorized-deduction exposure — counsel to advise: notice+consent, re-sign, or separate invoice.

HIGH · THIN18% GST on PAS commission + tax invoice

Only support is §3.4/§3.6 "exclusive of applicable taxes." Arguably puts GST on top of commission, but does not name 18%, does not state PAS will raise a GST tax invoice, and does not confirm the merchant can claim input tax credit. §25 runs the other way (merchant-side tax only). Reserved gstOnCommission column exists but is hardcoded 0.

Fix: Add to Taxes: "PAS's commission, onboarding fee, payment-processing fee and other charges are exclusive of GST; PAS shall levy GST at the applicable rate (currently 18%) and issue a valid GST tax invoice enabling input tax credit. PAS's GSTIN is [___]." CA to confirm SAC/rate + place-of-supply.

HIGH · THIN0.1% TDS §194-O withheld by PAS

Only support is the settlement "taxes, government deductions" catch-all — which doesn't name §194-O, doesn't oblige PAN furnishing (so the §206AA higher non-PAN rate isn't flagged), doesn't commit PAS to issue Form 16A, and the merchant never expressly consents. PAN is captured in the header (helpful), but the clause is missing. No TDS computation in the engine.

Fix: Add a clause: merchant acknowledges PAS is an e-commerce operator under §194-O and authorizes 0.1% deduction (or §206AA rate without valid PAN), agrees to furnish/maintain PAN, and PAS deposits the TDS + issues Form 16A for Form 26AS credit. CA to confirm the gross base + thresholds.

MEDIUMGST TCS §52 (0.5%) — not selected, but confirm it's genuinely not mandatory

Correctly absent operationally, but for a GST-registered e-commerce operator collecting consideration on registered sellers' taxable supplies, §52 TCS is commonly mandatory, not optional. Get the CA's written determination, and add a dormant enabling clause so PAS needn't re-paper every agreement if it later applies.

MEDIUMDining commission base — what 7% / 1.9% / GST / TDS attach to

§3.4 charges 7% on "orders originating through the Platform." Unclear whether, for a customer who only reserved via the Platform and pays in-restaurant (PAS not processing payment), 7% applies to the whole bill — and whether the gateway fee + §194-O TDS even apply to that non-Platform-processed leg. This sets the base every today-charge attaches to. CA to confirm.

Sequencing implication for the payout build: the engine work we scoped (deduct 1.9% gateway + 18% GST + 0.1% TDS) should not go live deducting from merchants until these charges are papered + re-consented. Build the math now; gate the live deduction on the agreement update.

2 · Signed text vs the live system (verified in code)

These are not drafting opinions — the reviewers traced them to specific code/migrations.

CRITICALLIVE EXPOSURECommission default (8%/10%) exceeds the signed restaurant/bakery rate (5%/7%)

The restaurant body promises 5% (pickup) / 7% (pickup+dining). But the settlement engine resolves the rate at close from the admin-editable commission_rules table, and the seed (migration 20260610170000) sets the NULL/untiered default for Restaurant/Bakery to 8%/10%. An untiered restaurant that signed 5/7 would be charged 8/10 — ~60%/43% above the only rate it ever saw. There's no code path linking the signed rate to the profile the engine reads. (Real settlements are in dry-run with no categories assigned yet, so this is a latent default, not money already taken — but it must be corrected before go-live.)

Fix (both sides): capture the signed commission rate onto the settlement profile at e-sign and have the engine prefer it, OR set the Restaurant/Bakery NULL-tier default to 5/7 so the safe default never exceeds the signed promise. Add §3.3A: "PAS shall not apply a rate higher than the accepted rate without the Merchant's acceptance of revised terms." Do not ship the current default.

HIGHContract says T+2 settlement; system settles weekly

§3.5/§3.7 promise settlement "initiated within two (2) business days" of completion. The cron runs 0 2 * * 1 (Mon 02:00 IST) and closes only the prior fully-elapsed week, with PAID a further manual step — routinely 6–13+ days, never T+2. A structural breach of the written promise for nearly every order; the "unless otherwise notified" hedge is too weak to cover a permanent weekly cadence.

Fix: Rewrite the §3.5/§3.7 timing to a weekly cycle with a stated post-close payout SLA (e.g. "close Monday, pay by Wednesday"), keeping the existing adjustments carve-out.

HIGHBakery signs ₹2,999 but is charged ₹999 (+ 8/10 default)

Bakeries route to the Restaurant agreement (states ₹2,999 onboarding) but onboarding is charged off Vertical.isPremium (₹2,999 only if premium, else ₹999), and bakery is not premium. So a bakery signs ₹2,999 yet pays ₹999, and inherits the 8/10 commission default. (This is forlater item B.)

Fix before any bakery signs: either make Bakeries premium (so the charged ₹2,999 matches the agreement) + seed its rule to 5/7, OR route bakeries to Other Stores (₹999 + 5%). The charged onboarding MUST equal the §3.1 figure in the signed agreement.

MEDIUMCommission-category matching is admin free-text

The engine matches a free-typed commissionCategory exactly against the rules table; a mismatch holds the order, a wrong-but-valid category silently charges another vertical's rate. The signed agreement's vertical is never consulted.

Fix: Derive the commission category deterministically from the signed agreement type at e-sign; normalize seed naming; add a referential check.

3 · Critical must-haves missing

CRITICALA real Data Processing Addendum (DPDP Act 2023)

The whole data surface is one undifferentiated paragraph (§22) + a thin confidentiality clause. "Data Fiduciary" / "Data Processor" appear nowhere; no documented-instructions duty, no sub-processing control, no audit right, no data-principal-rights assistance, no cross-border or retention/deletion terms. Breach notice is one-directional (Merchant→PAS) and silent on PAS's duty to notify the Data Protection Board + affected principals. DPDP processing-by-a-processor needs a valid contract — this is a placeholder, not compliance.

Fix: Add a DPA schedule: PAS = Data Fiduciary, Merchant = Processor on documented instructions; breach-notice + duty to assist PAS's Board/principal notification; sub-processor authorization + flow-down; audit; principal-rights assistance; cross-border restriction; return-or-delete on termination with a deletion certificate. Finalize fields with counsel against the in-force DPDP Rules.

CRITICALFSSAI: mandatory licence + consumer-display + PAS's own e-commerce FBO status

The FSSAI header field is optional (renders "—" when blank) and food verticals route by category name with no licence gate — a food merchant can sign with no FSSAI number. The FSS e-commerce framework requires each seller's FSSAI number to be displayed to consumers, and a food-facilitating platform is itself an FBO needing its own registration. §4.3's generic "food safety registrations where applicable" does not discharge these public-law duties enforced directly against PAS.

Fix: Add a Food-Vertical Schedule (hold valid FSSAI, furnish number/certificate before activation, authorize consumer display, notify on suspension); make the field mandatory/validated for food templates; and (PAS-entity action) confirm PAS holds the right FSSAI e-commerce FBO registration.

CRITICALPharmacy routed to the generic template — zero drug controls

"Pharmacy & wellness" routes to the generic Other Stores 5% template with no drug-licence requirement, no registered-pharmacist supervision, no Schedule H/H1/X prescription validation. Online pharmacy is heavily litigated with direct platform liability.

Fix: Either block pharmacy onboarding until a dedicated e-pharmacy framework exists, or add a Pharmacy Schedule (valid drug licence, registered-pharmacist dispensing, prescription validation, no banned drugs, PAS delist right). Route to a pharmacy-law specialist.

HIGHNo arbitration / dispute-resolution clause (yet §27 references it)

§21 gives only governing law + exclusive Hyderabad jurisdiction. There's no arbitration clause — but §27 (Survival) and the recital reference "dispute resolution," an internal inconsistency opposing counsel can exploit.

Fix: Add a Dispute Resolution clause as §34 (fills the existing 33→35 gap, resolves the dangling reference): good-faith negotiation → arbitration under the 1996 Act, Hyderabad seat, English, with a §9 interim-relief carve-out. Counsel must draft the arbitrator-appointment mechanism (unilateral PAS appointment is challengeable).

4 · Sector / licensing gaps

  • Legal Metrology / Packaged Commodities — no clause requiring MRP, net quantity, manufacturer details, country of origin, mfg/expiry on pre-packaged goods (grocery, packaged food, electronics, fashion, cosmetics). Absent.
  • FSSAI shelf-life-at-delivery — packaged food needs ≥30% shelf life / 45 days remaining at delivery; clauses ban only "expired products." Absent.
  • Age-restricted categories — no alcohol/tobacco/nicotine entry in Prohibited Products; no restaurant liquor/excise-licence warranty.
  • Seller-disclosure flow-through (CP E-Commerce Rules) — merchant not obliged to furnish legal name + address + GSTIN + grievance contact for consumer display.
  • Return/refund/warranty policy not incorporated — §8/§20/§33 defer to undefined "PAS policies" with no named/versioned/URL'd policy; a merchant signs without knowing the refund regime applied to its settlements.
  • Fair-ranking disclosure + PAS-side no-price-manipulation undertaking — only the merchant is bound (reviews); no reciprocal PAS undertaking.
  • Allergen display for all food sellers — restaurant §5.1 covers allergens; standard §5.1 does not, so meat/grocery packaged-food sellers have no allergen duty.
  • CCO / Nodal / Resident Grievance Officer — only a single Grievance Officer; if PAS crosses the SSMI user threshold, IT Rules 2021 require the three named India-resident roles. Counsel to confirm threshold.

5 · Enforceability

  • E-signature method. A finger-drawn signature is almost certainly not an "electronic signature" under IT Act §3A (which contemplates Aadhaar eSign / DSC), so it does not attract the Evidence Act §85B presumption. It can still be a valid §10A e-contract — but its weight rests on the audit trail (IP, device, timestamp, SHA-256 hash, click-acceptances) admitted via a §65B certificate. Reword §24 to rest on §10A + the audit record; ensure IP/device/timestamp are captured server-side; offer an Aadhaar eSign/DSC path for high-value relationships.
  • Stamp duty (Telangana) is unaddressed. An unstamped agreement is inadmissible in evidence until deficit duty + penalty are paid — the single most likely reason an otherwise-valid agreement fails at the admissibility stage, directly undercutting the §21 court-enforcement strategy. CA/local counsel to set the duty + e-stamping method.
  • Missing boilerplate: no Notices/address-for-service, no severability, no no-waiver, no mutual-amendment mechanism, no signatory-authority warranty, no insurance requirement (despite heavy food/allergen indemnity), no express books/audit right.
  • Unilateral amendment exposure: §3.6/§3.8 let PAS revise commercial terms on "reasonable prior notice" with no consent and no opt-out. More defensible B2B than B2C, but counsel should add a defined notice period + a no-penalty termination-on-adverse-change right, and consider carving the core commission rate out of unilateral revision.
  • Numbering defects: both bodies jump §33→§35 (no §34); the standard body has two "POLICY FRAMEWORK" sections (§20 + §33); §27 references a non-existent "dispute resolution" clause. Confirm with counsel before renumbering executed copies.
  • Recital party ID: "registered office in India" is generic; state the actual Hyderabad office + CIN/GSTIN.
  • Aadhaar: maskAadhaar() masks only the rendered display — confirm the full Aadhaar/PAN at rest is encrypted/access-controlled, and confirm the lawful basis for collecting Aadhaar at all (Aadhaar Act/UIDAI).

  • Add a §2.5 signatory-authority warranty + a signing-UI checkbox recorded in the audit block.
  • Reword §24 to rest on IT Act §10A + the audit record (waive "signed electronically" objection).
  • Add Notices, Severability, No-Waiver clauses (three structural gaps closed at once).
  • Amend §32 to state 48-hour acknowledgement + disposal within one month + a ticket reference number.
  • Make the FSSAI field mandatory/validated for food templates + a one-line activation precondition.
  • Add alcohol/tobacco/age-restricted entries to Prohibited Products + a restaurant liquor-licence line.
  • Insert Dispute Resolution as §34; fix the recital's registered office.

8 · What the agreements get right

  • Intermediary/marketplace status clearly established (§1.2–1.4) — a solid classification foundation.
  • Named Grievance Officer with full contact + postal address (§32), citing the IT Act 2000 + CP E-Commerce Rules 2020, with a 48-hour acknowledgement.
  • Real electronic-execution evidence: signed-at timestamp (IST), IP, device, SHA-256 doc hash, explicit Privacy/Terms/Partner click-acceptances — the right architecture for §65B/§10A.
  • Sensible data-minimization: Aadhaar masked to last-4 on display; PAN captured (supports TDS/GST mechanics once clauses are added).
  • Strong private-risk allocation: broad merchant indemnity (incl. food safety), a 3-month-commission liability cap, chargeback recovery, broad verification/suspension powers.
  • The restaurant body is thoughtfully differentiated (dining section, allergen/hygiene wording, two-tier commission, menus in the IP licence, 30-day termination).
  • Good engineering self-awareness: the bakery mismatch and the "reserved until CA confirms" tax columns are already flagged in code + forlater — gaps aren't papered over.
  • The onboarding fee correctly reads "plus applicable taxes" (§3.1) — the model for how every PAS charge should read.
